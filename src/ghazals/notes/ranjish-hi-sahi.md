"Ranjish hi Sahi" is among the most beloved Urdu ghazals of the twentieth century — a poem whose title alone has become shorthand for love that persists through estrangement. The phrase means roughly *"even if it must be in resentment"*: the poet asks the beloved to come back on any terms at all, even to quarrel, even only to leave again. Presence on any condition is preferable to absence.

### The poet and the singers

The ghazal was written by **Ahmad Faraz** (1931–2008), one of the most popular Urdu poets of his generation. It was *first* sung by **Iqbal Bano**, but it was **Mehdi Hassan** who made it immortal — his rendition for the 1972 Pakistani film *Mohabbat*, composed by Nisar Bazmi, is the version most people carry in their memory. Mehdi Hassan set it in **Raga Yaman**, and his slow, ornamented unfolding of the opening line is often cited as a high-water mark of the ghazal form.

### A note on the verses

Two of the couplets that Mehdi Hassan habitually sang — *"maana ki mohabbat ka chhupaana hai mohabbat"* and *"jaise tujhe aate hain na aane ke bahaane"* — were in fact written by **Talib Baghpati**, not Faraz. Mehdi Hassan wove them into his performances so consistently that they are now inseparable from the ghazal in popular memory, even though they don't appear in Faraz's original.

### On the word *ranjish*

It's worth dwelling on the title word. **Ranjish** is not rage or open hostility — it's the cooler, sadder thing: the quiet estrangement that settles between two people who were once close. The whole ghazal lives in that register of wounded tenderness rather than confrontation, which is why it aches rather than burns.

### Renditions worth hearing

- **Mehdi Hassan** — the definitive version; unhurried, deeply ornamented.
- **Iqbal Bano** — the original, with her characteristic dignity.
- **Ali Sethi (Coke Studio, 2017)** — a contemporary reading that introduced the ghazal to a new generation and drew tens of millions of listeners.

> Listening across these versions is its own small education in the form: the same words, the same raga, and yet each singer finds a different grief inside them.
