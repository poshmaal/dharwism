Few songs in the South Asian repertoire are as instantly recognisable as "Aaj Jaane Ki Zid Na Karo" — a tender plea to a beloved not to leave, to stay *just a little longer* against the certainty that the night, and perhaps everything, will end. Its refrain has become shorthand for the ache of impending parting.

### Not, strictly, a ghazal

A small but interesting point: although it is almost universally called a ghazal, this is really a **nazm** (or *geet*) — a continuous poem on a single theme — rather than a true ghazal with its independent couplets. The confusion is so common that at a memorial concert, an audience of three hundred admirers reportedly could not name its author when asked. It is kept here among the ghazals simply because that is how most singers and listeners encounter it.

### The poet and the composer

The words are by **Fayyaz Hashmi** (1920–2011), a prolific lyricist born in Calcutta who had already written hundreds of songs before migrating to Pakistan in 1947. The melody was set by the gifted composer **Sohail Rana**, in **Raga Yaman Kalyan** — the serene evening raga whose open, unhurried character suits the song's lingering mood perfectly.

### How it became immortal

It was **first sung by Habib Wali Mohammad** as a playback song for the Pakistani film *Badal Aur Bijli* (1973). By Farida Khanum's own account the film vanished from theatres within days, and the song might have gone with it — until she sang it at a private gathering, the audience fell for it, and word spread.

It was her slow, ceremonious rendition, recorded for Pakistan Television and performed at countless live concerts, that turned it into a classic. Farida Khanum became so identified with it that she is called **Malika-e-Ghazal**, the Queen of Ghazal, largely on its strength. In 2015, at the age of eighty, she sang it again for **Coke Studio**, introducing it to yet another generation.

### Worth listening for

- **Habib Wali Mohammad** — the original film version, lighter and more melodic.
- **Farida Khanum** — the definitive reading; listen for how she stretches the opening "aaj," and the famous sigh on "haaye."
- Later admirers have included Asha Bhosle and A. R. Rahman, though most listeners return to Farida Khanum.

> The genius of the song is in its restraint: it never names what is being lost. It only asks for one more night — and lets the listener supply the grief.
